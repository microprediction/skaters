FAQ

A short list, growing as questions recur.

Why is likelihood the default, not CRPS?

A skater's target is a reusable predictive density, and densities are meant to be chained: each stage refines the residual of the last. Held-out log-likelihood is the score under which those refinements compose, the log-likelihood of a chained forecast being the base score plus the contribution of each stage. CRPS is a good score for a different target, coverage or threshold behaviour, and it is reported alongside as a diagnostic. It is a different contract, not a worse one. The full argument, with the worked example and where CRPS is the right choice, is in why skaters ranks by likelihood, by default.

Isn't the benchmark win just p-hacked?

Reasonable thing to ask of any leaderboard. Rather than take one slice on faith, the robustness explorer lets you cut the non-price benchmark any way you like — at random, by category, frequency, stickiness, martingality — and watch the per-series win-rate recompute live with a bootstrap band. A cherry-picked edge falls apart under reslicing; a real one stays high and tight. The price caveat is stated plainly too: on equity/fx/commodity returns GARCH-t wins, and we say so.

Do I need exogenous data or features for this to be useful?

Not to use it. Univariate distributional prediction is a composable component, not a whole pipeline: it runs on the series you have and plugs in before, during, or after the rest of a model. Exogenous structure enters as another stage. See Scope.

Why not just build a bespoke model?

For a single important series, do. The value here is autonomy and scale: thousands of streams, online, unattended, each returning a full distribution rather than a point. See Scope.

Doesn't the transform leave the hard part, the time-dependence, exactly as hard? (the copula objection)

Yes, and that is the design rather than a defect. The forecaster is the time-dependence model: the conditional law it emits each tick is its running account of the serial structure, and z is what is left over after that account. So the claim is never that z is independent. The claim is that whatever dependence remains in z is precisely the improvement still available, priced in nats by exact change of variables, a measurement rather than an assumption. Run ARIMA, ETS, GARCH or Prophet on the transformed stream and they add a few hundredths of a nat on the series benchmarked so far (the front-end results log), which says the forecaster had left little serial structure behind on those streams, not that none could ever remain. The copula reading is exactly the architecture: margins and conditional dynamics from the forecaster, any dependence a second stage can still find modelled in z, with the log-likelihood accounting additive across the two stages.

"A nonlinear transform of an optimal estimate is usually not the optimal estimate of the nonlinear transform." Isn't this exactly that mistake?

The rule is E[g(X)] ≠ g(E[X]), and it bites whenever a point summary is pushed through a nonlinearity. Three exact facts put this machinery inside the rule's own exception rather than in its blast radius. First, distributions transform exactly where expectations do not: if Y has conditional law F, then F(Y) is uniform and the pullback of any density on z is exactly a density on y. Change of variables is an isomorphism of measures, so there is no Jensen gap in the transform itself; the only estimated object is F, same as in any model. Second, and this is the direct answer: the rule's stated exception is maximum likelihood, because likelihood is invariant under smooth invertible reparametrisation, and that exception is used here as the design principle. The whole apparatus is scored by log-likelihood, and the identity log p(y) = log f̂(y) + log q(z) − log φ(z) holds pathwise for the actual estimated f̂, however wrong it is. Estimation error in F therefore never becomes a silent bias inside a nonlinearity; it appears as its own explicit, measured term, the prequential miscalibration the parade tracks every tick. Third, when a point is finally wanted back in the original coordinates, it is computed as the expectation of the pulled-back distribution by quadrature, which is the optimal estimate of the transform, not the transform of an optimal estimate: the rule applied, not violated. And when we deliberately broke it, plugging a point through the inverse map instead, it failed exactly as the old advice predicts, a pathology the results log reports in its own footnote.

Online methods struggle to adjust parameters without a restart. How is that handled?

By never having a fit-then-freeze stage to restart. Every estimator in the tree is recursive with forgetting: exponentially weighted scales, recursive least squares with a forgetting factor, and a likelihood-weighted ensemble that reweights its candidate models on every tick, so a regime change demotes candidates instead of forcing a refit, and windup guards reset degenerate estimators locally. The cost of adaptation is monitored rather than hidden: the prediction parade computes prequential calibration diagnostics each tick, so parameter lag shows up as measurable miscalibration, and memory speed is an exposed knob rather than a buried constant. After a genuine structural break the forecaster does pay a re-adaptation cost, measured in the benchmarks, and that is the price the whole design accepts for running unattended at scale. State purity keeps the escape hatch cheap: any body can be checkpointed, forked, or restarted from its state dict.
